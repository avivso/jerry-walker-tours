Pont Royal: the pretty bridge with the less-polite history

![](blog/images/pont-royal.jpg)

Pont Royal may look calm and unassuming, but its history is a bit less polite.

The bridge was built in the late 17th century under Louis XIV, the Sun King, after its predecessor, a wooden bridge, collapsed again and again in floods. The king basically said: enough with the improvising, and switched to stone. Ever since, it has been one of the city's most reliable bridges.

At one point, a toll was charged here. Parisians, being Parisians, tried to dodge it; some even preferred the risk of taking a boat across at night rather than paying. The French, even back then, hated taxes.

And in the background? Not just a pretty building, but the Musée d'Orsay, a former railway station that became one of the world's most beloved museums. A place where trains gave way to Monet, and portraits replaced timetables.

A few words about Molière in Paris

![](blog/images/moliere.jpg)

Molière, France's greatest playwright, was born in Paris in 1622 as Jean-Baptiste Poquelin. He came from a well-established bourgeois family, and his father intended a respectable career for him at the king's court. But Molière chose a completely different path: the theater. In those days actors were considered fairly disreputable people, and the choice looked almost like social suicide.

Yet Molière became one of the people who redefined French culture. In plays like Tartuffe, The Miser, Don Juan and The Imaginary Invalid he mocked religious hypocrisy, the arrogance of the rich, doctors, aristocrats and the human weaknesses we all still recognize today. He was funny, sharp, cruel and brilliant all at once.

Not everyone loved it. The Church attacked him, courtiers took offense, and his plays often caused scandals. But Louis XIV admired him and protected him.

His death was almost too symbolic to be real. In 1673, while playing the lead in The Imaginary Invalid, a play about a man obsessed with illness and doctors, Molière collapsed on stage, by then genuinely ill and exhausted. He managed to finish the performance but died a few hours later at his home in Paris.

Even after his death he found no peace. Because he was an actor, the Church initially refused to give him a normal Christian burial, and only after heavy pressure and royal intervention was he buried at night and relatively discreetly.

Today Molière is buried in the Père-Lachaise cemetery in Paris, alongside Chopin, Edith Piaf, Oscar Wilde, Jim Morrison and other giants. Justice was done.

Love cheese? So do the French, and so did Charles de Gaulle

![](blog/images/fromage-degaulle.jpg)

Do you know the French concept of art de vivre? It isn't just "the art of living," it's a whole worldview in which food is not fuel but heritage. And at the heart of that heritage sits cheese. For the French, cheese isn't a supermarket-shelf product but a story of terroir: a total connection between the land, the climate, local tradition and the people who make it with sacred devotion. Every village in France is as proud of its cheese as if it were a work of art, and that's exactly why it became a symbol of their individualism and national pride.

General Charles de Gaulle understood this complex character of his people perfectly. In 1962, in the middle of an especially stormy period that included the end of the divisive Algerian war, assassination attempts against him and tough political struggles to stabilize the Fifth Republic, he let slip the immortal line that went down in history: "How can you govern a country that has 246 kinds of cheese?"

It wasn't just the complaint of a frustrated leader, but a brilliant metaphor. De Gaulle understood that you can't truly impose uniformity or discipline on a people in which every citizen and every region has its own unique taste, character and tradition that no one is willing to compromise on. Cheese was his way of describing a nation of millions of people who are all, ultimately, leaders in their own eyes.

But it turns out that since de Gaulle's day, the task has become much harder. If in 1962 the General was racking his brain over fewer than 250 kinds, since then the number of French cheeses has soared, and by today's official estimates it stands at more than 1,200 different kinds of craft cheese!

So next time you're trying to reach an agreement with a group of people and it feels impossible, think of whoever is trying to run a country with a thousand kinds of cheese, and take comfort.

The Great Synagogue of Paris: an architectural and historical gem

![](blog/images/synagogue-victoire.jpg)

The 9th arrondissement isn't only the Opéra Garnier. A short walk from the opera you'll find a corner of magnificent Jewish history in the heart of the City of Light.

If you're traveling in Paris, don't miss a visit to the "La Victoire" synagogue (Grande Synagogue de la Victoire). This spectacular building, with its enormous dome and Byzantine-Romanesque design, is far more than another tourist site: it is the beating heart of the Jewish community in France.

The synagogue was inaugurated in 1874, at a time when the Jewish community in Paris flourished. It was built as a symbol of the integration of Jews into French society, and became a place where the history of European Jewry was written.

Here, in this synagogue, Alfred Dreyfus stood under the wedding canopy at his marriage to Lucie Hadamard. Who would have believed that just a few years later, that same man would become a symbol of the fight against antisemitism and of the most famous trial in history.

Here Theodor Herzl, the visionary of the Jewish state, was called up to the Torah during his visits to Paris. It's hard not to be moved thinking that between these walls of velvet and gold, perhaps his first thoughts of Zionism took shape.

And it's impossible without a little traditional "Parisian" flair: one of the surprising things you'll encounter on a visit is the synagogue's beadle (shamash). When you look at him, you can't help but notice the special hat he wears: a classic bicorne, exactly like Napoleon Bonaparte's!

And I asked too: why the hat?!

Well, this is the community's special way of honoring the memory of Napoleon, who established the Consistoire, the official body that organized Jewish religious life in France at the start of the 19th century and gave France's Jews equal civil status. The beadle, in his splendid hat, is a living reminder of the deep and complex relationship between the Jewish community and the French state. I tell this story in depth on my tours.

What happened in Paris in the winter of 1870? The story the city prefers to forget

![](blog/images/siege-1871.jpg)

There are things about Paris that everyone knows: the scent of fresh baguettes, cafés along the Seine, art, elegance, light.

And then there is the story Paris prefers not to remember: the winter when the most refined city in the world ate its own elephants and horses.

No metaphor. Just history at its most brutal.

Between September 1870 and January 1871, Paris was completely surrounded by the Prussian army and cut off from the world. Two million people were trapped inside. No trains, no supplies, and rapidly, no food. First the cattle were slaughtered, then the pigs, then the poultry. Then came the horses, nearly 70,000 of them. And when even the horses ran out, Parisians turned toward the animals in the city's zoos.

In the Jardin des Plantes, two beloved elephants, Castor and Pollux, had long been symbols of Parisian pride. Children adored them; artists painted them. But by December 1870 no one had food for them, and no one had food for themselves. The unimaginable happened: both elephants were shot, butchered, and sold for sky-high prices, 40 francs per kilo, ten times the pre-war price of beef. Parisians lined up to buy elephant meat. In times of starvation, sentiment is a luxury no one can afford.

That same Christmas, the famous Voisin restaurant hosted what may be the most surreal holiday dinner in history. The menu included elephant consommé, camel fillet, kangaroo stew, bear cutlets, pigeon stuffed with kangaroo, and even cat served with a rat stuffed with chicken liver. Outside, cannons shook the city. Inside, by candlelight, Parisians toasted with champagne smuggled in by balloon, clinging stubbornly to the "art of dining," even as dining had turned into survival.

Almost the entire collection of animals from Paris's zoos was consumed: camels, ostriches, antelopes, kangaroos, bears, exotic birds, anything safe to butcher. The dangerous animals, lions and tigers, starved in their cages, victims of the siege like everyone else.

When the siege ended in January 1871, Parisians emerged into daylight as if awakening from another world. Paris had survived, but it never forgot. Even today, the expression "manger du chat," literally "to eat cat," remains a Parisian metaphor for extreme desperation, born from that bleak winter.

The animals eaten during the siege came mainly from two places that still exist today: the Jardin des Plantes in the 5th arrondissement, one of Europe's oldest zoos, and the Jardin d'Acclimatation in the Bois de Boulogne, now a family park that once housed camels, kangaroos, bears, and many others. These places still stand, quieter now, but full of echoes.

10 things about Louis XIV, the "Sun King", you didn't know

![](blog/images/louis-xiv.jpg)

The statue in the photo stands today in the courtyard of the Hôtel de Ville in Paris, but the man it commemorates was far greater than the statue, the palace, and perhaps even the state itself.

Here are ten surprising things about the man who ruled France longer than any other king:

1. He became king at just four years old.

When his father died in 1643, Louis was a small child. For years his mother and Cardinal Mazarin ruled in his name, but the experience made him suspicious of politicians and the nobility his whole life.

2. He survived fleeing an angry mob.

In his childhood a series of revolts known as the Fronde broke out. One night the royal family was forced to flee Paris. That memory made him hate the city and prefer Versailles.

3. He built Versailles in order to control the nobles.

Many think Versailles was built only out of a love of splendor. In practice, it was also a golden trap. The nobles were required to live near the king and compete for his attention instead of accumulating power in the provinces.

4. He probably never actually said "I am the state."

This is one of the most famous quotes in history, but there's no reliable evidence he ever said it. It may be a legend created after his death.

5. He used his morning as a political tool.

The king's waking ceremony was a state event. Nobles fought over the right to hand him his shirt, hold a candle or help him put on his shoes. Proximity to the king was power.

6. He was a semi-professional dancer.

Louis performed in royal ballets and even played the sun god Apollo. That's where the nickname "the Sun King" was born.

7. He suffered from serious health problems.

Despite his glamorous image, he underwent painful surgeries, suffered from many illnesses and lost most of his teeth relatively early in life.

8. Almost all his heirs died before him.

An enormous family tragedy befell him. His son, grandson and great-grandson all died before him. When he died in 1715, the crown passed to his five-year-old great-grandson, who became Louis XV.

9. In his time France became the superpower of Europe.

The French language, French fashion, French architecture and culture became a model imitated in almost every royal court in Europe.

10. He ruled for 72 years (far more than Bibi and Ben-Gurion combined).

This is one of the longest reigns in the recorded history of Europe. When he was born, many people around him still remembered the age of Shakespeare. When he died, the world already looked completely different.

And when I stand in front of this statue with travelers in Paris, I always ask them one question: was Louis XIV the greatest king of France, or the man who laid the foundations for the French Revolution that would erupt 74 years after his death?

The answer is far more complex than it seems.

The 19th-century department stores of Paris: architectural gems

![](blog/images/grands-magasins.jpg)

This week I again found myself going up to the rooftop of the Printemps Haussmann department store. The first times I came there like most visitors, for the view. It's hard to compete with the sight of the Opéra Garnier right across, the Eiffel Tower on the horizon, the gray zinc roofs of Paris and Montmartre peeking out to the north. But the more I return, the more I realize that the truly interesting story isn't only what you see from the roof, but the building you're standing on.

Printemps, whose name means "spring" in French, was founded in 1865 out of a brilliant marketing idea: to create a place associated with renewal, fashion, freshness and a sense of promise. No longer a dark, crowded shop, but a modern palace of shopping, beauty and experience. It was born right inside Baron Haussmann's new Paris, a city of wide boulevards, uniform facades, lighting, transport, an enriching bourgeoisie and an almost religious faith in progress.

It wasn't alone. A few years earlier Le Bon Marché had opened, in 1852, considered the first modern department store in the world. After it came BHV in 1856, opposite the city hall. Printemps joined in 1865, La Samaritaine was founded in 1870 near the Pont Neuf, and toward the end of the century, in 1893, Galeries Lafayette was born. Together they created an entirely new genre: not a market, not a small shop, not a family boutique, but a whole world under one roof.

The revolution wasn't only in size. These department stores changed the very way people shopped. They introduced fixed prices instead of haggling, lavish display windows, seasonal sales, catalogs, the ability to wander freely between departments, and sometimes even to return goods. In the 19th century this was a new consumer freedom, especially for middle-class women, who could enter a magnificent public space, look, choose, compare, dream, and not necessarily buy right away.

That's why architecture was part of the sale. The domes, the iron, the glass, the stained glass, the staircases and the light pouring from above were not innocent decoration. They were meant to make the visitor feel they were entering a place larger than life. Printemps itself went through fires, expansions and rebuilding, but kept the same basic idea: the store is not only a place where goods are sold, but a stage on which the Parisian dream is displayed.

The concept is almost identical in all these historic department stores: a magnificent building, many departments, luxury brands, restaurants, cafés, impressive architecture and sometimes a rooftop or observation terrace. These weren't just shops; they were the new cathedrals of the 19th century. Instead of making a pilgrimage to centers of religion and nobility, the new middle class made a pilgrimage to the temples of fashion, consumption and beauty.

And what's charming is that the idea still works. Even today, on the upper floors of these stores, you can find restaurants and bars where Parisians sit. They come to eat, to drink, to feel for a moment part of the luxury, and above all to enjoy access to the most beautiful observation terraces in the city. Sometimes it seems the food is only part of the experience; the real ticket is the chance to sit above Paris, look down on it, and feel the whole city spread out before you.

And that, to me, is Paris at its best: even when you think you've gone up to the roof of a department store, you discover you're standing inside a whole chapter in the history of architecture, fashion, class, commerce and dreams. On brocante culture and second-hand markets in Paris

![](blog/images/brocante.jpg)

What do Parisians do on Sundays? No, most of them don't go to church. They go out hunting for bargains.

Almost every weekend, dozens of brocantes and vide-greniers take place across the city, from the elegant ones in the 16th arrondissement to the more local and popular ones in the east. For many Parisians, wandering between the stalls with a coffee in hand is a classic Sunday pastime, just like brunch or sitting in a park.

But there's an important difference between the two.

A brocante is a market usually attended by professional antique and second-hand dealers. You'll find furniture, silverware, porcelain, old books, posters, jewelry and collectibles there. The downside is that the sellers usually know exactly what they're selling, and the prices match.

A vide-grenier, which literally means "emptying the attic," is a completely different story. Here it's the neighborhood residents themselves who open up storerooms, basements and attics and sell things they no longer need. It's precisely here that you can find the real bargains: an old book for two euros, silverware sold at a ridiculous price, an original painting, a vintage watch or a collector's item the seller isn't even aware of the value of.

After quite a few visits to both types, I've learned that if your goal is not only to stroll but also to find a little treasure at a bargain price, the best chances are actually at a vide-grenier. Sometimes you have to go through dozens of stalls to find something special, but that's exactly the magic: you can never know what will turn up at the next stall.

And that may be one of the most Parisian things there is: to take your time, wander with no fixed destination, and come home with a good story and a find you weren't even looking for.

Montmartre: the story behind the Sacré-Cœur

![](blog/images/sacre-coeur.jpg)

When I arrive with travelers at the stairs leading up to the Sacré-Cœur ("the Sacred Heart"), I tell them, among other things, that this famous building was born out of one of the bitterest disputes in France's history.

Most visitors see a beautiful church on an impressive hill, photograph the view, and that's it. I think few people know that its construction wasn't just an architectural or religious project. It was a political and ideological statement meant to answer a question that occupied France for decades: what kind of country does France want to be?

To understand the Sacré-Cœur you have to go back to the turbulent years after the French defeat to Prussia in 1870.

The empire of Napoleon III (about whom I dig deep on my tours) collapsed, the army was defeated, Napoleon III himself was taken prisoner (!), Paris was placed under siege, and many in France felt the country was on the verge of collapse.

But the crisis didn't end with the war. In 1871 the Paris Commune was active: a short-lived revolutionary attempt to establish a popular, anti-clerical and quite radical government. For many of France's conservative and Catholic elites, the Commune wasn't just a political revolt but a sign that the nation had lost its way morally and spiritually.

Out of that feeling, the idea was born to build, on the hill of Montmartre, a basilica dedicated to the Sacred Heart of Jesus. The project's initiators believed (so I want to believe) that the defeats, violence and chaos that befell France were the result of straying from faith and Christian values. In their eyes, building the church was an act of national atonement and spiritual renewal. But in the eyes of their opponents, especially the republicans and secularists, it was a political gesture meant to restore the Church's influence over French society and to erase the memory of the Commune (I relate more to these claims).

That's why the Sacré-Cœur is far more than a tourist site. It's a monument to a deep argument about France's identity. Is France the daughter of the Catholic Church, or the daughter of the French Revolution? Does her future lie in religious tradition or in the values of secularism and the republic? It seems that even today this argument is still going on, more than a hundred and fifty years after the first cornerstone was laid.

Maybe that's why I love so much to bring travelers here. Behind one of the most photographed images in Paris hides a story far more complex than that of a white dome and a stunning view. It's a story about war, ideology, faith, revolution and historical memory. And as often happens in Paris, the moment you scratch a little beneath the surface, you discover that the most famous place is sometimes also the most contested.

A scandal in the heart of Paris in the middle of the 17th century

![](blog/images/st-germain-auxerrois.jpg)

One of the greatest scandals of Paris actually took place in the quiet church opposite the Louvre.

In 1662, a man who was already the biggest star of French theater entered the church of Saint-Germain-l'Auxerrois: Molière. He was 40, famous, close to King Louis XIV, admired by many and hated by no fewer. Beside him stood his young bride, Armande Béjart, who was only about twenty. Supposedly just another Paris wedding. In practice, one of the most talked-about scandals of the 17th century.

Almost immediately the whispers began. For years Madeleine Béjart, the bride's mother, had been Molière's partner, a brilliant actress and one of the most important women in his life. But when he married Armande, rumors spread across the city that the young bride was not Madeleine's sister, as the family claimed, but actually her daughter. If the rumors were true, it meant Molière had married someone who grew up in the home of his former lover, and perhaps even her daughter. No one managed to prove it then, nor in the 360 years since, but Paris fell in love with the story and the gossip didn't subside until the day he died.

But fate had an even more dramatic ending in store for Molière. In 1673, while performing the lead role in his play The Imaginary Invalid, he collapsed on stage after a severe coughing fit. The audience at first thought it was part of the show. A few hours later he died at his home on Rue de Richelieu, a short walk from the church where he was married.

And here begins perhaps the saddest chapter of his story. Even though he was the most famous writer and playwright in France, the Church at first refused to grant him a full Christian burial. In those days actors were considered, in the eyes of the Church, members of a morally questionable profession. The man who made an entire nation laugh, who got to perform before the king himself and whose works became classics, was about to find himself without a dignified burial.

Only after a special intervention by King Louis XIV (in whose court Molière was a regular) was he allowed to be buried, and even then at night, in a modest ceremony without the honor due to his status. Many years later his remains were moved to the Père-Lachaise cemetery, where he rests today alongside other great French artists.

Every time I pass Saint-Germain-l'Auxerrois with travelers, I remind them that this church isn't just another beautiful Gothic building. Here one of the great gossip affairs of Paris began. Here Molière was married. A few hundred meters from here he died. And between these two events hides a story of love, rumors, fame, jealousy, religion, politics and the power of society to judge people, a story that feels remarkably modern even today.

And I still haven't told you about another drama connected to this place that happened a hundred years earlier, more on that in a separate post.

Where did I eat for my birthday, and why?

![](blog/images/le-procope.jpg)

Last week I celebrated a birthday. When you live in Paris, it's not a simple choice. This city has more than 40,000 restaurants, from a small neighborhood bistro to Michelin-starred restaurants. And yet, when that special evening came, I found myself returning to one unique place: Le Procope.

I didn't choose the restaurant because it serves the best steak in Paris, nor because it's especially trendy. I chose it because there are places where you don't just eat dinner. You eat inside history itself.

Le Procope was founded in 1686, and is considered the oldest continuously operating café and restaurant in Paris. While the Land of Israel was deep under Ottoman rule, and America hadn't yet dreamed of independence, here sat thinkers, philosophers, poets, politicians and revolutionaries discussing the future of France.

When you step inside, it's hard not to feel it. The heavy carpets, the chandeliers, the antique mirrors, the books, the portraits and the narrow corridors create a sense of entering a time tunnel. In every corner a story hides.

One of the most intriguing objects in the place is the black hat displayed in the cabinet. It's a reconstruction of the famous hat associated with Napoleon Bonaparte. Beneath it lies an open book describing the events of November 1799, the coup of the 18th Brumaire, the day Napoleon put an end to the French Revolution and began his path as ruler of France. It's a defining moment in European history, and the restaurant chose to commemorate it precisely here, in a place where Napoleon himself used to dine.

They say that during the time he was a young, penniless officer, Napoleon used to eat at Le Procope on credit. He would come here, satisfy his hunger, and lacking the means, leave his hat at the restaurant as a deposit until payment. Apparently he didn't quite remember to pay.

Later, when he became Emperor of France, he returned to the place as one of the most powerful people in the world. It's perhaps one of the most beautiful stories about the long road between a dream and reality.

But Napoleon wasn't the only one. Voltaire was a regular here. They say he used to drink dozens of cups of coffee a day. Rousseau sat here. Denis Diderot came here. Benjamin Franklin met here with French intellectuals during the time he tried (and succeeded) to recruit France for the American Revolution. Danton, Marat, Robespierre and the figures of the French Revolution sat here, argued, planned and sketched out the future of the nation.

In short, we ordered a classic French-style steak, with fried potatoes and good wine. Traditional French food, without gimmicks and without trying to impress. Everything was well made. But the truth is that the food, excellent as it was, was only part of the experience.

The real pleasure was to sit at ease, look around, and imagine all the people who sat here over the past 340 years. Kings, revolutionaries, generals, poets, writers and philosophers. People who believed they were changing the world, and sometimes really did.

In a city where every corner tells a story, Le Procope is not just another restaurant. It's living testimony that history isn't always written in palaces, on battlefields or in parliaments. Sometimes it's written around a small table, beside a cup of coffee, among friends arguing about a new idea. So if you're going to celebrate a birthday in Paris, it's hard to think of a more fitting place.

The cliffs of Étretat: the end of the world? Maybe the end of Europe

![](blog/images/etretat.jpg)

Just two hours from Paris lies one of France's most breathtaking natural wonders, the white cliffs of Étretat. Towering over the turquoise sea, these dramatic arches and chalk formations have inspired artists for centuries, from Monet and Boudin to modern photographers seeking that perfect light.

Walk along the coastal path, feel the ocean wind on your face, and watch how the colors shift every minute, from deep blue to shimmering silver.

It's nature's own masterpiece, carved by time and tide.
